Why Do We Need Filters?

<span key="8eca042336824a2a81a6d5871fa9e7dc"><span offset-key="8eca042336824a2a81a6d5871fa9e7dc:0"><span slate-zero-width="n">​</span></span></span>

### 

<span key="d0375dcb41e14250990d60607d35c97e"><span offset-key="d0375dcb41e14250990d60607d35c97e:0">Why Do We Need Filters?</span></span>

<span key="725d1e0c21534ac9b5dfd34b1da0c464"><span offset-key="725d1e0c21534ac9b5dfd34b1da0c464:0">Filters are used in a wide variety of applications. Most of the time, the final goal of using a filter is to achieve a kind of frequency selectivity on the spectrum of the input signal.</span></span>

<span key="2ed8bb2d0de8425eadc931a4f82a089d"><span offset-key="2ed8bb2d0de8425eadc931a4f82a089d:0">As an example, suppose that a 50-Hz noise falls on top of the signal produced by a sensor. The noise component may be strong enough to limit the measurement precision. The output of the sensor is usually converted to a digital signal by an ADC to be processed by a DSP or a microcontroller. Therefore, we can use a digital filter after the ADC to eliminate the noise component. In this particular example, a notch filter centered at 50 Hz can be utilized to suppress the noise.</span></span>

### 

<span key="baf1f15e80d24db1935bbcec135feb29"><span offset-key="baf1f15e80d24db1935bbcec135feb29:0">General Considerations</span></span>

<span key="bedb2c46369d47f8a752877932a3cb2b"><span offset-key="bedb2c46369d47f8a752877932a3cb2b:0">Digital filter design involves four steps:</span></span>

<span key="6e7f4a0d896245a0bdd7933c0492afdc"><span offset-key="6e7f4a0d896245a0bdd7933c0492afdc:0">1) </span>**Determining specifications**</span>

<span key="74b96d5f39f94c488af16120f85b0882"><span offset-key="74b96d5f39f94c488af16120f85b0882:0">First, we need to determine what specifications are required. This step completely depends on the application. In the example of 50-Hz noise on the output of the sensor, we need to know how strong the noise component is relative to the desired signal and how much we need to suppress the noise. This information is necessary to find the filter with minimum order for this application.</span></span>

<span key="26597f09adcc4ef6992cd4dee384bed4"><span offset-key="26597f09adcc4ef6992cd4dee384bed4:0">2) </span>**Finding a transfer function**</span>

<span key="500e7f74fbab4b62bb1fb0ada419a3e9"><span offset-key="500e7f74fbab4b62bb1fb0ada419a3e9:0">With design specifications known, we need to find a transfer function which will provide the required filtering. The rational transfer function of a digital filter is as in Equation (1).</span></span>

<span key="c3bdf9af2f0a46b5bf22638697c531b0"><span offset-key="c3bdf9af2f0a46b5bf22638697c531b0:0">H(z)=∑M−1k=0bkz−k∑N−1k=0akz−kH(z)=∑k=0M−1bkz−k∑k=0N−1akz−k</span></span>

<span key="0dcfdb47646d47bcaf9e44ce886339c0">**Equation (1)**</span>

<span key="c2b3856fff72472c8365adec1dacdd1e"><span offset-key="c2b3856fff72472c8365adec1dacdd1e:0">This step calculates the coefficients, akak and bkbk, in Equation (1).</span></span>

<span key="84e4df896f504714a70189c6351883bc"><span offset-key="84e4df896f504714a70189c6351883bc:0">3) </span>**Choosing a realization structure**</span>

<span key="7508837b5dc04c1085ac50ac572b3bd8"><span offset-key="7508837b5dc04c1085ac50ac572b3bd8:0">Now that H(z)H(z) is known, we should choose the realization structure. In other words, there are many systems which can give the obtained transfer function and we must choose the appropriate one. For example, any of the direct form I, II, cascade, parallel, transposed, or lattice forms can be used to realize a particular transfer function. The main difference between the aforementioned realization structures is their sensitivity to using a finite length of bits. Note that in the final digital system, we will use a finite length of bits to represent a signal or a filter coefficient. Some realizations, such as direct forms, are very sensitive to quantization of the coefficients. However, cascade and parallel structures show smaller sensitivity and are preferred.</span></span>

<span key="94b2008b9af44b0fbfac611001225d27"><span offset-key="94b2008b9af44b0fbfac611001225d27:0">4) </span>**Implementing the filter**</span>

<span key="775d02aeec7d4bd5b61a57228df58101"><span offset-key="775d02aeec7d4bd5b61a57228df58101:0">After deciding on what realization structure to use, we should implement the filter. You have a couple of options for this step: a software implementation (such as a MATLAB or C code) or a hardware implementation (such as a DSP, a microcontroller, or an ASIC).</span></span>

<span key="c042115bd0764bc5880adcea40447fda"><span offset-key="c042115bd0764bc5880adcea40447fda:0">This article focuses on the second step in designing an FIR filter.</span></span>

<span key="aedb293f48cd4753a7c371265419a710"><span offset-key="aedb293f48cd4753a7c371265419a710:0">The reader may wonder why a linear-phase frequency response is important. To gain insight, consider the continuous-time case. Assume that the frequency response of a system is</span></span>

<span key="edb4fc3d3e8b4f13abe69a5ff28f1861"><span offset-key="edb4fc3d3e8b4f13abe69a5ff28f1861:0"><span slate-zero-width="n">​</span></span></span>

<span key="8de8905206a04dcd8d211ca70a88781d"><span offset-key="8de8905206a04dcd8d211ca70a88781d:0">This article will review some basic structures to implement Finite Impulse Response (FIR) filters.</span></span>

<span key="1e8d52ab3c5540cfbcc5535352a65dcc"><span offset-key="1e8d52ab3c5540cfbcc5535352a65dcc:0">When designing a filter, we start from a set of specifications which are generally determined by a particular application. For example, we may need to suppress the 50-Hz noise which appears at the output of a sensor and makes our measurements inaccurate. Based on the frequency and strength of the noise component, we can determine the filter specifications such as the passband and stopband edge, the transition bandwidth, the ultimate out-of-band rejection, etc. Now, we can find the system function, H(z)H(z), which satisfies these requirements.</span></span>

<span key="c01cfbc209024a5b9ee0f74675788cc1"><span offset-key="c01cfbc209024a5b9ee0f74675788cc1:0">For a FIR design, we have a couple of options such as </span></span><a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/technical-articles/finite-impulse-response-filter-design-by-windowing-part-i-concepts-and-rect/" class="css-4rbku5 css-1dbjc4n r-1loqt21 r-1471scf r-1otgn73 r-1i6wzkk r-lrvibr"><span data-key="a5f4abce1c0343178e3facc62ff2331e" data-rnw-int-class="nearest_257-5120_259-5121-237__"><span data-key="ec15f046edae4e83a86da629c43b313a"><span data-offset-key="ec15f046edae4e83a86da629c43b313a:0">the windowing method</span></span></span></a><span key="d571595617b942b988def14245c06159"><span offset-key="d571595617b942b988def14245c06159:0">, the frequency sampling method, and more. Then, we need to choose </span></span><a href="http://www.ee.ic.ac.uk/hp/staff/dmb/courses/DSPDF/01000_Structures.pdf" class="css-4rbku5 css-1dbjc4n r-1loqt21 r-1471scf r-1otgn73 r-1i6wzkk r-lrvibr"><span data-key="48607fe404e84c249bb54b14eada0373" data-rnw-int-class="nearest_257-5120_259-5121-237__"><span data-key="4ed2b3dc26e34dcbbe6208ed62f642d0"><span data-offset-key="4ed2b3dc26e34dcbbe6208ed62f642d0:0">a realization structure</span></span></span></a><span key="82e45456000644b6b4c397e16e590888"><span offset-key="82e45456000644b6b4c397e16e590888:0"> for the obtained system function. In other words, there are several structures which exhibit the same system function H(z)H(z). One consideration for choosing the appropriate structure is the sensitivity to coefficient quantization. Since a digital filter uses a finite number of bits to represent signals and coefficients, we need structures which can somehow retain the target filter specifications even after quantizing the coefficients. In addition, sometimes we observe that a particular structure can dramatically reduce the computational complexity of the system.</span></span>

### 

<span key="76667a23504340b488a5a7c09c5a2683"><span offset-key="76667a23504340b488a5a7c09c5a2683:0">The Cascade-Form Structure</span></span>

<span key="b785c52850364dc6bf2f2a7309364cad"><span offset-key="b785c52850364dc6bf2f2a7309364cad:0">While the direct form is derived from the difference equation, the cascade structure is obtained from the system function H(z)H(z). The idea is to decompose the target system function into a cascade of second-order FIR systems. In other words, we need to find second-order systems which satisfy</span></span>

<span key="f7c738663bb8443fa2037a3467698b14"><span offset-key="f7c738663bb8443fa2037a3467698b14:0">H(z)=M−1∑k=0bkz−k=P∏k=1(b0k+b1kz−1+b2kz−2)H(z)=∑k=0M−1bkz−k=∏k=1P(b0k+b1kz−1+b2kz−2)</span></span>

<span key="46d4cfae7c854d7ca0ae4d09064b508b">**Equation 2**</span>

<span key="30e7f46c36f948909cd11cc44cc22ce2"><span offset-key="30e7f46c36f948909cd11cc44cc22ce2:0">where PP is the integer part of M2M2. For example, M=5M=5, H(z)H(z) will be a polynomial of degree four which can be decomposed into two second-order sections. Each of these second-order filters can be realized using a direct form structure. It is desirable to set a pair of complex-conjugate roots for each of the second-order sections so that the coefficients become real. The reader may wonder why we need to rewrite a given H(z)H(z) in terms of second-order sections. In a future article, we will see that the main advantage of the cascade structure is its smaller sensitivity to the coefficient quantization.</span></span>
